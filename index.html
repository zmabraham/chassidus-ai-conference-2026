Volume II · 2026

The revolution, quietly continuing.

Last summer, a group of developers, educators, and curious learners gathered for the first time around a question that, until very recently, would have sounded strange even to ask: what does it mean for the deepest teachings of Chassidus to be read, translated, and explained by a machine?

The answers, as it turned out, were less strange than the question. People are already doing it. They are building tutors that walk a student through a difficult ma'amar, translation tools that handle the dense Hebrew-Aramaic-Yiddish layering of a Chassidic text, visualization platforms that map abstract spiritual concepts into something a teenager can hold in their head.

This year we return — to see what the year produced, to hear from the people doing the work, and to think together about where this is going. The day is short, the room is small, and the conversations have, in our experience, a way of becoming the year's most interesting.

On precedent

Why this moment.

In each generation, the Chabad-Lubavitch movement has taken hold of the newest medium of its day and turned it toward Torah.

Movable type put printed sefarim into the hands of ordinary householders. Radio carried farbrengens across continents. Television brought the Rebbe's words into living rooms in real time. The early internet, against the instincts of its skeptics, proved to be one of the most consequential platforms for Jewish outreach the world has known.

Artificial intelligence is the medium of the next decade. The work — to translate, to teach, to bring the inner dimensions of Torah to the next person and the further place — continues.

Practical matters

Frequently asked.

Who is this for?+

Anyone seriously interested in the intersection of Chassidus, technology, and Jewish education — developers, teachers, students, rabbinic leaders, and curious learners. There is no formal background requirement.

Is there a cost to attend?+

No. The conference is free of charge. Registration is required, and we ask that you only register if you genuinely intend to attend, since virtual seats are limited.

How do I propose a presentation?+

Use the "Submit a proposal" link above. Tell us what you've built or are building, what it is for, what stage it is at, and what you would need around 8–12 minutes on stage to show. We read every submission.

Will the sessions be recorded?+

Yes. As with last year, all registered attendees will receive full recordings, with captions, in the days following the conference.

What language is the conference in?+

Primarily English, with Hebrew, Aramaic, and Yiddish source material referenced as it comes up. Recordings are captioned in English.

Is there a code of conduct?+

Yes. We expect respectful, generous, and honest engagement from speakers and attendees alike. The full text is sent with the calendar invitation upon registration.
